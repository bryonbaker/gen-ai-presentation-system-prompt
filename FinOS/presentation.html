Why Financial AI Requires a Different Operating Model

Understanding the unique risks of deploying AI in regulated financial environments

The Problem

Financial AI introduces new risk categories

Financial AI must be evaluated differently from consumer AI.

Model Risk

General-purpose LLMs struggle with financial domain reasoning:

  • Interpreting earnings reports and SEC filings
  • Analysing financial statements
  • Numerical reasoning in investment analysis

Errors directly affect risk models, trading decisions, and financial reporting.

Operational Risk

AI agents autonomously perform financial tasks:

  • Retrieving market data
  • Analysing financial news
  • Generating investment insights

Non-deterministic behaviour requires continuous monitoring and auditability.

Regulatory & Compliance Risk

  • Training data provenance is unclear
  • Licensing restrictions are unknown
  • Outputs cannot be audited by regulators

Governance Gap

Today there is no widely adopted framework for benchmarking financial AI, evaluating agents in production, or ensuring transparency and compliance.

The Problem

The core challenge

Financial institutions deploying GenAI face three critical questions:

🔍

Which models can we trust?

How do we evaluate financial reasoning capability and accuracy?

⚙

How do we safely operate AI agents?

How do we monitor autonomous systems in production?

📋

How do we meet audit requirements?

How do we ensure regulatory compliance and model transparency?

This presentation outlines a framework addressing all three.

Governing Financial AI Systems

Operational evaluation and model transparency

Governance

AgentOps: Evaluating AI agents in practice

Two layers of governance for the AI agent lifecycle.

1

Development Governance (Inner Loop)

"Glass box" evaluation of agent behaviour during development.

  • Reasoning validation
  • Tool usage testing
  • Context adherence
  • Safety guardrails
2

Operational Governance (Outer Loop)

Continuous evaluation of AI systems in production.

  • Decision tracing
  • Audit logs
  • LLM-as-a-Judge safety scoring
  • Human-in-the-loop escalation

Production failures are fed back into training datasets to continuously improve reliability. This closes the loop between development and operations.

Governance

Model Openness Framework

Avoiding "open-washing" in AI model procurement.

Many models claim to be open but hide training datasets, licensing terms, and development processes.

Class Level of Openness What's Shared
Class III Open Model Partially open components: model weights, card, evaluation results
Class II Open Tooling Training code, evaluation code, inference code, and tools
Class I Open Science Fully open: data, code, parameters, research papers, documentation

This enables financial institutions to select models based on transparency, auditability, and compliance readiness — not just benchmark performance.

What This Means for Banks

Practical governance layers for financial AI deployment

For Financial Institutions

Three governance layers for financial AI

Banks deploying GenAI should implement three governance layers.

1

Model Evaluation

Benchmark models against financial datasets. Can the model reason about financial statements? Does it hallucinate in regulatory documents?

2

Agent Monitoring

Track AI agents operating in production. Decision tracing, audit logs, escalation procedures, and runtime evaluation of agent behaviour.

3

Model Transparency

Deploy only models meeting transparency requirements. Training data provenance, licensing clarity, reproducibility, and regulatory auditability.
